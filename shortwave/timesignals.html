TIME SIGNALS

Radio stations that literally tell the time.

Time-signal stations are one of the coolest corners of radio because they blend science, engineering, history, and listening culture all in one place. They are not ordinary broadcasters. They exist to transmit highly accurate time and frequency references that people, clocks, instruments, and radio listeners can use.

For beginners, they are amazing targets because they are famous, meaningful, and useful. For experienced listeners, they are perfect reference signals and part of radio’s deeper technical history.

What are time signals?

Radio time signals are broadcasts designed to carry accurate time and frequency information. Some are meant for direct listening, with spoken announcements or tones. Others are designed for clocks and receivers that automatically decode the signal.

In simple terms, they are radio stations you can use as a precision reference. They help people set clocks, check receivers, monitor propagation, and verify that equipment is tuned correctly.

  • They provide official or highly controlled time references
  • They often transmit standard frequencies for calibration
  • They are useful for both hobby listening and technical work
  • They are some of the most iconic signals in radio

Why they matter to listeners

Time stations are brilliant because they turn a listening session into something concrete. Instead of hoping to stumble on a random station, you can tune to a known frequency and immediately learn something useful: whether the band is open, how well your antenna is performing, and whether your tuning is accurate.

They also just sound cool. The tones, ticks, announcements, and coded signals feel deeply “radio.” They are technical, but also weirdly atmospheric.

Famous radio time-signal stations

These are some of the best-known time and frequency stations you can build pages around.

UNITED STATES

WWV / WWVH

WWV and WWVH are among the most famous radio time stations in the world. WWV is based in Colorado and WWVH in Hawaii. They transmit standard time and frequency information on multiple shortwave frequencies.

  • 2.5 MHz
  • 5 MHz
  • 10 MHz
  • 15 MHz
  • 20 MHz
WWV Page Shortwave
CANADA

CHU

CHU is Canada’s official shortwave time station. It continuously broadcasts time-of-day information and is a fantastic target for listeners because it is both useful and historic.

  • 3330 kHz
  • 7850 kHz
  • 14670 kHz
CHU Page Shortwave
UNITED KINGDOM

MSF

MSF is the UK’s dedicated standard-frequency and time broadcast. It operates on longwave rather than shortwave and is widely used by UK radio-controlled clocks.

  • 60 kHz
Longwave Clock Signals
JAPAN

JJY

JJY is Japan’s standard time and frequency signal service. It uses low-frequency broadcasts that clocks and receivers can decode.

  • 40 kHz
  • 60 kHz

Best beginner targets

For shortwave listeners, WWV and CHU are especially nice because they are classic, useful, and directly part of HF listening culture. For longwave and radio-controlled-clock curiosity, MSF and JJY are great related rabbit holes.

How to listen for time signals

The easiest way is to treat them as named targets rather than random frequencies. Pick one known service, tune carefully, and listen with patience. If you are on shortwave, WWV and CHU are lovely places to start.

  • Use a receiver with stable tuning
  • Try different times of day on shortwave
  • Use headphones if the signal is weak
  • Log what you hear and when

Why they are so useful

Time stations are not just neat curiosities. They let you test your setup, compare conditions, and hear a truly meaningful signal. If you can copy a known time station clearly, that tells you a lot about your receiver, antenna, and the state of the band.

They are also part of radio’s deeper culture. Hearing one connects you directly to a long tradition of standard-frequency and clock services that supported science, communications, and daily life.

Propagation DXing

A little history

Time and frequency radio services became important because they gave people and equipment a way to receive a reliable standard reference over the air. That made them useful for synchronization, calibration, science, navigation, engineering, and everyday clock-setting.

REFERENCE

Frequency standards

Some time stations are valued not only for the time announcements themselves, but because the carrier frequency is tightly controlled and can be used as a reference.

BROADCAST USE

Direct listening

Services like WWV, WWVH, and CHU are famous partly because human listeners can tune in and immediately hear something meaningful rather than just raw coded pulses.

CLOCK USE

Automatic decoding

Services like MSF and JJY are closely tied to radio-controlled clocks and receivers that decode the signal automatically.
