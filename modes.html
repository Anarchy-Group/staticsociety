RADIO MODES

AM, FM, USB, LSB, CW, and digital modes explained.

Radio modes are the different ways audio, data, or information are carried over a radio signal. Some modes are mainly used for broadcasting, some are used for voice communication, and others are designed for Morse code or digital data. Understanding radio modes makes it much easier to know what you are hearing and why a signal sounds the way it does.

Mode comparison

This table gives a quick overview of the main radio modes and what makes each one different.

Mode Full Name Main Use Strengths Limitations
AM Amplitude Modulation Broadcasting Simple, widely understood, easy to receive More affected by noise and interference
FM Frequency Modulation Broadcasting and local comms Clearer audio and better noise rejection Usually more line-of-sight and local in range
USB Upper Sideband HF voice communication Efficient and narrow compared with AM Sounds strange if tuned incorrectly

Digital Various digital modes Text, data, weak-signal work Can work very well under difficult conditions Often needs software and decoding tools

Good rule of thumb

AM and FM are the most familiar broadcast modes, USB and LSB are common for voice communication on HF, CW is Morse code, and digital modes are used for machine-readable communication like text and data.

BROADCAST MODE

AM

AM stands for Amplitude Modulation. In AM, the strength of the carrier signal changes to carry audio information. This is one of the oldest and most familiar radio modes and is strongly associated with medium wave broadcasting.

  • Commonly used for medium wave broadcasting
  • Easy to receive with simple radio equipment
  • Can travel long distances, especially at night
  • More vulnerable to static and electrical noise
BROADCAST MODE

FM

FM stands for Frequency Modulation. Instead of changing signal strength, FM changes the frequency slightly to carry audio. This gives FM better sound quality and makes it less sensitive to noise than AM.

  • Commonly used for VHF broadcast radio
  • Usually clearer sounding than AM
  • Good resistance to static and electrical noise
  • Often more local or regional because of line-of-sight behavior
VOICE COMMUNICATION

USB

USB means Upper Sideband. It is one form of single sideband, where only one sideband of the signal is transmitted rather than both sidebands and a full carrier like AM. This makes it more efficient in both bandwidth and power.

  • Common on many HF amateur and utility frequencies
  • More efficient than full AM transmission
  • Often used for long-distance voice communication
  • Needs accurate tuning or voices will sound wrong
VOICE COMMUNICATION

LSB

LSB means Lower Sideband. Like USB, it is a single sideband mode. The difference is that the lower sideband is transmitted instead of the upper one. In practice, USB and LSB are extremely similar except for tuning and band convention.

  • Also common on HF amateur radio bands
  • Often chosen based on operating convention
  • Efficient for voice communication
  • Needs a receiver capable of SSB reception
MORSE CODE

CW

CW stands for Continuous Wave. In radio hobby use this usually means Morse code. CW is extremely narrow in bandwidth and can be effective even when signals are weak, which is one reason it remains respected in amateur radio.

  • Used for Morse code communication
  • Excellent under weak signal conditions
  • Uses much less bandwidth than voice modes
  • Requires practice and decoding skill
DATA & TEXT

Digital Modes

Digital radio modes cover many systems used for text, data, images, telemetry, and weak-signal work. These include modes that rely on software to encode and decode information, often with much better performance in poor conditions than normal voice communication.

  • Can be used for text, data, telemetry, and weak-signal work
  • Often decoded with computer software
  • Very useful for experimentation and technical radio work
  • Includes many different formats with different purposes

USB vs LSB

Sideband can confuse beginners, so here is the simple version.

Both USB and LSB are forms of single sideband, often shortened to SSB. They work by removing part of the signal that would normally be sent in standard AM, which saves bandwidth and power. The main difference is which sideband is being transmitted.

In practice, many operators choose USB or LSB based on band convention. A listener does not usually think of one as “better” than the other in general. What matters is tuning the receiver correctly and using the same sideband as the transmitting station.

Broadcast modes vs communication modes

Not every mode is used for the same purpose.

  • AM and FM are closely associated with broadcasting
  • USB and LSB are more associated with two-way communication
  • CW is commonly used for Morse code
  • Digital modes are often used for text, data, or highly efficient communication

That is why identifying a mode helps explain what kind of station you may be hearing: a broadcaster, a ham operator, a utility station, or a machine-readable signal.

Listening tips

These are simple things that make a big difference when trying to identify radio modes.

Listen to how it sounds

AM usually sounds familiar but noisier, FM sounds cleaner, and badly tuned sideband often sounds unnatural or “Donald Duck-like” until tuned correctly.

Check the band

The frequency range gives useful clues. Broadcast FM is usually in VHF, medium wave usually means AM, and HF voice activity may often be USB or LSB.

Use the right receiver mode

Some radios let you switch between AM, FM, USB, LSB, and CW. Choosing the right one is essential for hearing the signal properly.

Frequently asked questions

A nice little FAQ section makes the page feel more complete and useful.

Why does sideband sound weird?

Sideband depends on correct tuning. If your radio is slightly off frequency, voices can sound too high, too low, or just very strange.

Is FM always better than AM?

FM usually sounds cleaner for local listening, but AM can travel farther in some situations, especially on lower frequencies and at night.

Do I need a special radio for USB and LSB?

Yes, your receiver needs to support single sideband reception. Basic broadcast radios often do not.

What mode should a beginner learn first?

AM and FM are the easiest starting point. After that, USB and LSB are a great next step if you want to explore amateur radio or HF communications.
