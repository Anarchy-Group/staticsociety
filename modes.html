RADIO MODES

AM, FM, USB, LSB, CW, digital modes, and where to tune on shortwave.

Radio modes are the different ways audio, data, or information are carried over a radio signal. Some modes are mainly used for broadcasting, some are used for voice communication, and others are designed for Morse code or digital data. Understanding radio modes makes it much easier to know what you are hearing and where you should start tuning.

Mode comparison

A quick overview of the main radio modes and what makes each one different.

Easy way to think about it

AM and FM are the familiar broadcasting modes, USB and LSB are the classic HF voice communication modes, CW is Morse code, and digital modes are used for text, data, and specialised communication.

BROADCAST MODE

AM

AM changes the strength of the carrier signal to carry audio. It is strongly associated with medium wave broadcasting and classic radio listening.

  • Used in broadcast radio
  • Easy to receive
  • Can travel long distances at night
  • More affected by static and interference
BROADCAST MODE

FM

FM changes the signal frequency slightly to carry audio. It usually sounds cleaner and rejects noise better than AM.

  • Common on VHF broadcast radio
  • Cleaner sound than AM
  • Strong resistance to static
  • Usually local or regional
VOICE COMMUNICATION

USB

USB means Upper Sideband. It is a type of single sideband used a lot for HF voice communication because it is more efficient than full AM.

  • Common on many HF frequencies
  • Efficient in bandwidth and power
  • Popular for long-distance voice
  • Needs accurate tuning
VOICE COMMUNICATION

LSB

LSB means Lower Sideband. It works just like USB, but uses the lower sideband instead of the upper one.

  • Common on HF amateur bands
  • Efficient like USB
  • Chosen by band convention
  • Requires SSB-capable equipment
MORSE CODE

CW

CW is usually how radio hobbyists refer to Morse code transmission. It uses very narrow bandwidth and works well even with weak signals.

  • Great for weak-signal work
  • Very narrow bandwidth
  • Classic radio skill
  • Requires Morse knowledge
DATA & TEXT

Digital Modes

Digital modes cover text, data, images, telemetry, and weak-signal modes that are often decoded with software.

  • Used for text and data
  • Excellent for experimentation
  • Often decoded by software
  • Includes many different formats

Tuning tip

Lower shortwave frequencies are often nicer to explore at night, while higher shortwave bands tend to open up better during the day.

Important note

On shortwave, the exact station you hear on a given frequency can change with season, time of day, target region, and current schedule. That’s part of the fun.

Famous time-signal frequencies

These are some of the best-known shortwave frequencies in the world and they are fantastic for testing reception.

  • 2.5 MHz / 2500 kHz
  • 5 MHz / 5000 kHz
  • 10 MHz / 10000 kHz
  • 15 MHz / 15000 kHz
  • 20 MHz / 20000 kHz

These are iconic because WWV and WWVH use them to transmit time and frequency information.

Listening tips

Simple things that make a big difference when identifying modes and finding stations.

Listen to how it sounds

AM usually sounds familiar but noisier, FM sounds cleaner, and badly tuned sideband often sounds strange until tuned correctly.

Check the band

The frequency range gives clues. Medium wave usually means AM, while HF voice activity may often be USB or LSB.

Use the right receiver mode

If your radio supports AM, FM, USB, LSB, and CW, changing mode is essential for hearing a signal properly.

Frequently asked questions

A nice little FAQ section makes the page feel more complete and useful.

Why does sideband sound weird?

Sideband depends on correct tuning. If your radio is slightly off frequency, voices can sound too high, too low, or just very strange.

Is FM always better than AM?

FM usually sounds cleaner for local listening, but AM can travel farther in some situations, especially on lower frequencies and at night.

Do I need a special radio for USB and LSB?

Yes. Your receiver needs to support single sideband reception. Basic broadcast radios often do not.

What mode should a beginner learn first?

AM and FM are the easiest starting point. After that, USB and LSB are a great next step if you want to explore HF and amateur radio.
